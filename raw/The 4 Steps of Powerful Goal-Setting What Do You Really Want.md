# **The 4 Steps of Powerful Goal-Setting: What Do You Really Want?**
A different way of approaching goal-setting — from the inside out
[Crystal Cha](https://crystalcha.medium.com/?source=post_page-----4f9ef25507c--------------------------------)

Via <a href="https://unsplash.com/@treesoftheplanet" rel="noopener" class="external-link" target="_blank"><u>Niklas Weiss</u></a> on Unsplash.com
What Tony Robbins, Danielle LaPorte, and Jon and Missy Butcher taught me about setting goals
This year, I started listening to Tony Robbins. Before this, I simply put him into the same bucket as other charismatic motivational speakers. Then I watched his documentary I Am Not Your Guru, and found myself surprised by how down to earth he seemed.
Coincidentally, I was recently invited to a talk by an acquaintance who had recently attended one of Tony Robbins’ seminars. He explained what he learned from concepts popularized by Tony Robbins, such as The Two Fears Everyone Has, The Six Human Needs, The Success Cycle, Peak State, and The Wheel of Life.
I learned that without awareness, we allow our goals to be driven by our fears. If we allow our fears and inaccurate beliefs to drive us, this affects the actions we take and results we will have, leading to a downward spiral.
The success spiral, on the other hand, is found by doing the opposite. When we make conscious decisions not to be led by our fears, this causes us to take actions that help us achieve our potential. As a result, we see the fruits of our actions that come from doing the right things, and they spur us to continue along that path.
I found that learning those concepts came at the perfect timing, just as I was about to embark on a process of goal-setting for the year ahead.
Later that week, I decided to dust off a book I had bought a few years ago, The Desire Map: A Guide to Creating Goals with Soul, written by Danielle LaPorte. The premise of Danielle LaPorte’s work is that achievement in itself leaves us feeling empty once we attain it, if we don’t know the real reasons why we wanted those things. Often, the reasons for our goals come from external sources. We want to be liked, recognized, appreciated, and so on. LaPorte teaches in her books that we should instead ask ourselves what we really want to feel. Then, we make goals based on what we need to do to feel that way.
She doesn’t specifically mention it, but she draws from concepts of counseling that differentiate feelings from emotions. We can’t always help what emotions we experience, but we can consciously choose how we want to feel. Via <a href="https://counseling.online.wfu.edu/blog/difference-feelings-emotions/" rel="noopener" class="external-link" target="_blank"><u>Wake Forest University</u></a>: “A fundamental difference between feelings and emotions is that feelings are experienced consciously, while emotions manifest either consciously or subconsciously.”
A few weeks before this, I was receiving Facebook ads from Mindvalley, promoting a free webinar with Jon and Missy Butcher. The Butchers are two entrepreneurs who are also grandparents but whose appearances and lifestyle seem to belong to people in their twenties. In the webinar, Jon and Missy Butcher briefly touched on their background story of watching three brothers die from drug abuse. This spurred them towards a journey to live a life of purpose.
The webinar introduced the course that Jon and Missy Butcher designed called LifeBook, or a process for designing and getting the life you want. The LifeBook process teaches concepts of having a more empowering view of money, the Twelve Categories of LifeBook, or a roadmap for your life, and The Four Questions to Ask and Answer in Each LifeBook Category.
After awhile, these teachings began to sound the same…
I began to spot recurring themes between what Tony Robbins, Danielle LaPorte, and Jon and Missy Butcher were teaching.
Tony Robbins’ seminars and coaching can cost anywhere from a few thousand to over ten thousand dollars. The LifeBook course costs $500. Desire Map workshop participants pay several hundred dollars to attend in-person or online workshops, and to become a workshop facilitator costs $2,000 to be part of the licensing program.
Yet all these inspiring people give away their wisdom for free, in the form of podcasts and blog articles, and affordably, through their books. Sure, the courses provide structure, the payment for them provides an incentive to participants to commit to the process, and for people who need community to work through these kind of life-altering transformations, the other attendees providing a supportive environment.
But this wisdom is easily accessible to anyone who wants to learn it. There is nothing unique about what these people teach. They have simply managed to codify and brand principles and concepts that have always existed about goal-setting and achieving your goals.
## **The 4 steps for goal-setting that top self-improvement leaders teach**
I’m going to summarize what these people taught me in a few simple steps:

1. Take stock of your life
Tony Robbins offers a free assessment you can take called the <a href="https://core.tonyrobbins.com/wheel-of-life-4/" rel="noopener" class="external-link" target="_blank"><u>Wheel of Life</u></a>. The Wheel of Life has seven sections: Health, Emotions, Relationships, Time, Work and Mission, Finance, and Spirituality and Contribution. Danielle LaPorte has the 5 Areas of Life and worksheets for auditing yourself in these five areas: Livelihood and Lifestyle, Body and Wellness, Creativity and Learning, Relationships and Society, and Essence and Spirituality. Jon and Missy Butcher have TWELVE LifeBook categories which I won’t list — but as you can guess, they more or less cover similar aspects of life.

The five areas of life from The Desire Map
Basically, the point is this: Audit every area of your life. Quantitatively give yourself a score. Qualitatively ask yourself how you feel about each area of your life. Ask the hard questions. Leave no stones unturned. Pinpoint where you have been compromising. Identify which areas are thriving. Discover what you are grateful for in each area, and what you are dissatisfied with in each area. Figure out what needs improving.
2. Determine what you really want.
The next step after the audit is using the new information and awareness you have gained to ask yourself what you truly want in every area of your life. In which areas of your life have you been living for the expectations of others and society instead of yourself? What do you really want in those areas? Are there areas of your life where you’ve never really taken the time to ask yourself what you want? This is exactly the stage in the process to do that.
3. Make a plan of action
Now you know what you truly want, based on where you truly desire to see growth and improvement. Instead of just setting some arbitrary goals, it’s time to make a plan. Figure out and write down in black and white what you want to commit to.
What things will you put into action? What major decisions do you need to make? What small, daily rituals might you need to start practicing? What things need to come to an end for what you want to happen? It could be a job, a relationship, a way of thinking. This is where you figure out what is the hard work that needs doing.
4. Commit to a rhythm of checking in with your plan
Making the plan of action is not the end. The hard part is actually doing it. This is where discipline, commitment, and accountability come it. The motivation that comes at the beginning of setting a new goal will fade very quickly. So this part is all about foreseeing that and putting in place check-ins with yourself to evaluate your progress.
Different teachers may recommend different time intervals — perhaps weekly, monthly, or quarterly — to check in with your goals. Tony Robbins talks about the importance of <a href="https://www.tonyrobbins.com/ask-tony/priming/" rel="noopener" class="external-link" target="_blank"><u>daily priming</u></a> to stay focused on your goals. Danielle LaPorte has weekly and monthly worksheets for checking in with your goals. Jon and Missy Butcher’s LifeBook process also talks about a routine of checking in.
The exact goals of course, can evolve and change along the way as you get closer to understanding and achieving a higher level of what you really want. It could be meaning, purpose, or connection. The point here is that we are forgetful and easily distracted. Creating rituals to help us stay focused are essential. Without them, all the other steps are futile.
***
And there you have it — the 4 steps that are part of all goal-setting processes taught by top self-improvement leaders. Hopefully, this has armed you with some tools to set more meaningful goals for the decade ahead, instead of arbitrary New Year’s resolutions that you are going to break before the end of January. Have a meaning-filled year ahead!
